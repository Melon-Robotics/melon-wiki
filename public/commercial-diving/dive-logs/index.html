Dive Logs & Operational Records

Traceability, auditability, and operational documentation requirements for dive operations

Dive logs and operational records provide traceability and auditability for commercial diving operations. This page covers what must be recorded, how records must be maintained, and why this matters for regulatory compliance and incident investigation.

Why This Exists#

Operational records serve multiple purposes:

  • Regulatory compliance — Required by IMCA, ADCI, and national regulations
  • Incident investigation — Essential for understanding what happened and why
  • Insurance claims — Required documentation for insurance coverage
  • Operational improvement — Historical data informs future planning
  • Legal defense — Proper records protect operators in disputes

What can go wrong: Incomplete records, lost records, altered records, untraceable records. Each failure mode creates legal and operational risk.

Who This Is For#

  • Dive supervisors maintaining records
  • Safety officers auditing record-keeping
  • Regulators reviewing compliance
  • Incident investigators reconstructing events
  • Insurance underwriters assessing risk
  • Legal counsel defending operations

Required Records#

Dive Log Entries#

Each dive must be logged with:

  • Date and time — Start time, bottom time, surface time, completion time
  • Location — Geographic coordinates, water depth, structure/work site
  • Diver information — Diver name, certification level, experience
  • Depth profile — Maximum depth, depth vs. time profile
  • Gas information — Gas mix, consumption, pressure readings
  • Decompression — Decompression schedule, stops completed, deviations
  • Work performed — Tasks completed, equipment used, issues encountered
  • Environmental conditions — Current, visibility, sea state, temperature
  • Team composition — Supervisor, standby diver, support personnel
  • Equipment — Equipment used, equipment issues, maintenance performed

Traceability: Each entry must be timestamped and attributable to a specific person. Records must be immutable once created.

Equipment Records#

Equipment must be tracked:

  • Inspection records — Pre-dive and post-dive inspections, periodic maintenance
  • Calibration records — Gas analyzers, depth gauges, communication equipment
  • Maintenance records — Repairs, replacements, modifications
  • Failure records — Equipment failures, near-misses, incidents

Audit-worthiness: Equipment records must demonstrate that equipment was maintained in accordance with manufacturer recommendations and regulatory requirements.

Training Records#

Personnel qualifications must be documented:

  • Certifications — Diver certifications, supervisor qualifications, medical fitness
  • Training — Training completed, competency assessments, refresher training
  • Experience — Dive history, hours logged, types of work performed

Responsibility: Operator maintains training records. Auditors verify that personnel are qualified for assigned roles.

Incident Reports#

Incidents, near-misses, and anomalies must be documented:

  • Incident description — What happened, when, where, who was involved
  • Causal analysis — Why it happened, contributing factors
  • Response actions — What was done in response
  • Preventive measures — What will prevent recurrence

Legal sensitivity: Incident reports may be discoverable in legal proceedings. They must be factual and accurate.

Record-Keeping Requirements#

Timeliness#

Records must be created:

  • During operations — Real-time logging of dive parameters
  • Immediately after — Post-dive debrief and log completion
  • Within 24 hours — Final log entry and supervisor review

What can go wrong: Delayed logging leads to incomplete or inaccurate records. Memory degrades quickly; records must be contemporaneous.

Accuracy#

Records must be accurate:

  • Measured values — Depth, time, pressure from calibrated instruments
  • Observed values — Environmental conditions, work performed, issues encountered
  • Attributed values — Who recorded what, when

What can go wrong: Estimation instead of measurement, rounding errors, transcription errors. Accuracy matters for decompression calculations and incident investigation.

Completeness#

Records must be complete:

  • All required fields — No missing information
  • All dives logged — No skipped dives
  • All incidents reported — No unreported incidents

What can go wrong: Incomplete records create gaps in operational history. Gaps reduce audit-worthiness and complicate incident investigation.

Immutability#

Records must be immutable once created:

  • No alterations — Corrections must be documented as amendments, not changes
  • Version control — Track all changes with timestamps and attribution
  • Audit trail — Who changed what, when, why

Legal requirement: Altered records are not credible in legal proceedings. Immutability is essential for audit-worthiness.

Data Provenance#

Records must demonstrate data provenance:

  • Source — Where did the data come from? Instrument, observation, calculation?
  • Method — How was the data obtained? Direct measurement, estimation, calculation?
  • Uncertainty — What is the uncertainty in the measurement?
  • Chain of custody — Who handled the data, when, how was it transferred?

Audit-worthiness: Data provenance enables auditors to verify record accuracy and identify potential errors or fraud.

Storage & Retention#

Storage Requirements#

Records must be stored:

  • Secure storage — Protected from loss, damage, unauthorized access
  • Backup systems — Redundant storage to prevent data loss
  • Access control — Only authorized personnel can access records
  • Format — Readable format that will remain accessible over time

What can go wrong: Lost records, corrupted files, inaccessible formats, unauthorized access. Storage systems must be reliable and secure.

Retention Requirements#

Records must be retained:

  • Regulatory minimums — IMCA, ADCI, and national regulations specify retention periods
  • Legal requirements — Statutes of limitation may require longer retention
  • Operational value — Historical records inform future operations

Typical retention: 5-7 years minimum, longer for incidents or legal proceedings. Retention policies must be documented and followed.

Digital vs. Paper Records#

Digital Records#

Advantages:

  • Searchability — Easy to search and filter
  • Backup — Easy to create redundant copies
  • Analysis — Easy to analyze trends and patterns
  • Access — Easy to share with authorized personnel

Challenges:

  • Format obsolescence — Formats may become unreadable over time
  • System dependencies — Requires software and hardware to read
  • Security — Requires cybersecurity measures
  • Integrity — Requires cryptographic verification to prevent tampering

Paper Records#

Advantages:

  • Permanence — Readable without technology
  • Legal acceptance — Well-established legal precedent
  • Simplicity — No software dependencies

Challenges:

  • Storage — Physical space requirements
  • Searchability — Difficult to search large volumes
  • Backup — Requires physical copying
  • Damage — Vulnerable to fire, water, deterioration

Operational reality: Most operations use digital records with paper backup for critical documents. Hybrid approaches are common.

Audit Requirements#

Records must support audits:

  • Regulatory audits — IMCA, ADCI, national regulators
  • Client audits — Contract compliance verification
  • Insurance audits — Risk assessment and claims verification
  • Internal audits — Quality assurance and continuous improvement

Audit-worthiness: Records must be complete, accurate, traceable, and immutable. Auditors must be able to verify operational compliance.
